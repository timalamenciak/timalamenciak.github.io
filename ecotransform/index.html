About

EcoTransform is a 48-hour hackathon at the University of Waterloo where teams of coders, ecologists, and designers build AI tools for biodiversity restoration and conservation. Participants tackle a connected pipeline of challenges — from extracting structured knowledge out of scientific literature, to building evidence-backed agents, to designing interfaces that put that evidence in the hands of practitioners in the field.

Teams will have access to two compute resources: the Nibi supercomputer at the University of Waterloo — one of Canada's most energy-efficient HPC systems — and an NVIDIA DGX Spark on-site for local GPU inference and fine-tuning workloads.

The event emphasizes sustainable AI: we award a dedicated prize for the solution with the smallest environmental footprint. Tools built to protect nature should themselves tread lightly.

Team Size

Teams of 2–6 people. You may apply as an individual and be matched to a team, or apply as a group. Interdisciplinary teams are strongly encouraged and will be prioritized.

Tracks

EcoTransform is structured as three interconnected tracks — the output of each feeds into the next. Two scheduled connection points over the weekend bring teams together to coordinate across tracks.

Track 1

Evidence Extraction Pipeline

Given a corpus of 308 restoration ecology studies (pre-converted to Markdown), build a pipeline that extracts structured ecological relationships and outputs them in KGX format compatible with EcoWeaver. Teams receive gold-standard annotated papers as examples and optimize for extraction accuracy.

CS-heavy · ecologists as domain experts
Track 2

RAG Agent

Given a KGX ecological knowledge graph built from grassland restoration literature, build an AI assistant that retrieves relevant subgraphs for practitioner queries, synthesizes evidence-backed statements, provides provenance, and avoids hallucination outside the graph.

Balanced CS + ecology
Track 3

Practitioner Query Interface

Build an interface where a restoration practitioner can describe their site — ecosystem type, geography, restoration goal — and receive relevant evidence from the GraphRAG agent, presented as readable summaries with provenance.

Design-heavy · CS for implementation

Awards

Three award categories recognise excellence, collaboration, and sustainability. Awards are distributed across tracks and the event as a whole.

🥇
3 AWARDS · ONE PER TRACK

Gold Raccoon — Best Solution

Awarded to the team delivering the strongest overall solution in their track. Judged on technical execution, creativity, and real-world impact potential by a panel of industry and academic experts.

🤝
1 AWARD · EVENT-WIDE · PARTICIPANT VOTE

Crystal Raccoon — Best Collaborator

Voted on by all participants on Sunday evening. Awarded to the team that most enriched the weekend through cross-track collaboration, knowledge-sharing, and generosity with their work.

🌿
3 AWARDS · ONE PER TRACK

Green Raccoon — Most Sustainable Solution

Awarded to the team whose solution best demonstrates sustainable AI practices: efficient compute, thoughtful model selection, minimal environmental footprint, and responsible use of the Nibi and DGX Spark infrastructure. Teams include a sustainability statement in their final presentation.

The Space

EcoTransform takes place in the DRAGEN Lab — the Medieval Digital Research in Arts and Graphical Environmental Networks Laboratory — at St. Jerome's University on the University of Waterloo campus. Founded in 2016, the DRAGEN Lab is a purpose-built digital humanities research space that deploys a collaborative, learner-centred approach to knowledge production across disciplines.

The lab occupies 3,600 ft² (242 m²) within the newly renovated St. Jerome's University Library. It was built to support exactly the kind of work EcoTransform demands: expert and student researchers working side-by-side in teams, with access to high-performance computing, digital fabrication tools, and flexible collaborative space. It also includes a Makerspace with professional-grade 3D scanning, 3D printing, and high-performance computing units — a fitting home for a hackathon pushing the edges of AI-assisted ecological knowledge.
