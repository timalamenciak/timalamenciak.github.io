Imagine this scenario: Your community group works for years restoring a local ecosystem. You spend hundreds of hours writing about your methods and what you've learned. You publish a booklet about how you did it. That booklet gets downloaded a few times and sits on your website.
Community groups ebb and flow. Maybe the champion leading the charge gets ill. Maybe there is some internal dispute. The web hosting lapses and the document disappears from the internet. 
That knowledge is special. It represents lived experience - thousands of hours restoring a landscape that **you** know better than anyone else. And that knowledge is gone.
Imagine a second scenario: You are a young person in a low-income community. You feel a connection to a local park - you want to do something to fix it. You can't afford to go to school or to buy pricey books. The local library doesn't carry any books on ecological restoration. The only available manual disappeared from the internet months ago. Nobody mirrored the PDF.

## Ecological restoration needs to embrace Creative Commons

There is a solution: Creative Commons licensing. It can help preserve knowledge and expand access. It would allow that community group to publish a document that could be archived in public repositories. It would allow that young community member to find information online rather than behind institutional barriers.
The goal of ecological restoration is to restore ecosystems. Specifically the goal is to do so in a way that is [effective, efficient and engaging](https://www.iucn.org/content/ecological-restoration-protected-areas-principles-guidelines-and-best-practices). Creative Commons provides a way to amplify the impact of restoration knowledge by allowing people to freely distribute new knowledge. Without a CC license, it's not technically legal to print and distribute a document yourself, or to mirror it on your website.

## Why bother licensing at all?

Of course community groups aren't likely issuing cease-and-desist orders - but that's not the point. 
The point is that a CC license gives explicit permission to share and distribute something. It's the difference between leaving something at the curb and hoping someone takes it or putting a sign on it that says "Free." People might take the stuff from your curb, but the sign makes it much more clear.

## What if somebody tries to sell my work? 

The CC license gives you options. As a creator you can specify whether your work can be shared for any purpose or strictly non-commercial ones. 

## What if someone claims they wrote it?

The CC license specifies that anyone who shares it must do so with the original attribution. Your group will still get credit for the hard work you did producing that knowledge.

## Okay, how do I sign up? 

Using a CC license signals to the world that you want your information to be shared. That is an important signal in ecological restoration - the impact that restorationists can have is amplified when information is easily shared.
Actually employing the license is easy - you can download an image from the [Creative Commons website](https://creativecommons.org/licenses/) and embed it in your publication. There are a few different license options to choose from (I typically stick with CC-BY - the most open license).
By licensing your restoration knowledge with Creative Commons you are contributing to a pool of open knowledge. That knowledge will be shared, mirrored and distributed to help grow the practice and knowledge of restoration globally.
